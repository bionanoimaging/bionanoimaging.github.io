LVL1_TASK7_CIRCULAR_FRINGES - Circular Fringes (Haidinger Fringes) Narration Script
===================================================================================

[Entering wave optics territory]
With both beams perfectly overlapped, we now enter the truly fascinating realm 
of optical interference. The pattern you see on the screen tells you everything 
about the relationship between the two arms of your interferometer.

[Types of fringes]
There are two main types of fringe patterns you can create in a Michelson 
interferometer. Straight parallel fringes appear when one mirror is slightly 
tilted relative to the other. Circular fringes - also called Haidinger fringes 
or fringes of equal inclination - appear when the mirrors are exactly perpendicular.

[What circular fringes mean]
When you see concentric rings centered on the screen, this indicates that the 
two mirrors form a perfect virtual air gap. Each ring represents rays that have 
traveled the same total distance in both arms. The geometry is analogous to 
contour lines on a topographic map, but here the "elevation" is optical path length.

[The center spot significance]
The very center of the circular pattern marks the condition of zero optical path 
difference. At normal incidence - where light travels straight out and back - 
both arms have exactly equal lengths. This is the most important reference point 
in the entire interferometer.

[Fine-tuning requirements]
Achieving perfect circular fringes requires exquisite angular alignment. You'll 
need to use very fine control over Mirror 2's tip and tilt. Even thousandths of 
a degree matter here. In this task, your rotation range is intentionally limited 
to ±1 degree so you focus on precision rather than gross adjustments.

[Observing the pattern]
In premium mode with interference enabled, you should see bright and dark rings 
that get closer together as you move away from the center. The spacing tells you 
about the path difference gradient across the beam profile.

[Why this matters scientifically]
Circular fringes are used in precision metrology for measuring refractive indices, 
detecting surface irregularities, and even searching for tiny changes in distance. 
The famous Michelson-Morley experiment that searched for the luminiferous ether 
used precisely this kind of fringe pattern analysis.

[Mastery indicator]
Being able to reliably produce clean circular fringes is a hallmark of skilled 
optical alignment. It shows you understand not just how to make things work, but 
how to make them work optimally.
