LVL1_TASK5_DIVerging_LENS - Diverging Lens Insertion Narration Script
======================================================================

[Introducing a crucial component]
Now we add the diverging lens, which is absolutely essential for precision 
alignment of the interferometer. This simple optical element unlocks a powerful 
technique used by professional optical technicians.

[What the lens does]
A plano-concave or biconcave lens spreads out light rays that pass through it. 
Instead of parallel rays staying parallel, they diverge from a common origin 
point. In our case, we want the laser beam to spread as it travels toward the 
beamsplitter.

[Why we need divergent light]
With perfectly parallel light, interference fringes appear at ANY mirror 
separation. You can't tell where the zero path difference position is because 
fringes look the same whether the arms are equal or very different in length.

But with divergent light, different rays travel different distances depending 
on their angle. At most positions, these various path differences cancel each 
other out, washing out the interference pattern. Only when the two arms have 
exactly equal lengths do ALL the rays interfere constructively together.

[Insertion instructions]
The lens should be placed between the laser and the beamsplitter, roughly along 
the optical axis around position [-2, -5]. You'll need to move it laterally 
until it intercepts the beam, then rotate it so both surfaces are perpendicular 
to the beam direction.

[What you should observe]
After inserting the lens correctly, notice how the beam expands as it travels. 
You'll see the ray bundle get wider downstream of the lens. This spreading is 
exactly what we need for the calibration technique.

[The alignment power]
This setup lets you find the exact zero optical path difference position. When 
you scan one mirror while watching the screen, fringes will only become visible 
in a narrow region around the equal-path condition. That bright fringe region 
marks your reference point with extreme precision.

[Looking ahead]
We'll use this property in Task 8 to demonstrate how professional metrologists 
find the absolute reference position before making precise measurements.
