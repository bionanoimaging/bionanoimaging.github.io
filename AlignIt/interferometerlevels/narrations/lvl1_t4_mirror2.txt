LVL1_TASK4_MIRROR2 - Second Mirror (Scanning Mirror) Narration Script
======================================================================

[Introduction to the second arm]
Mirror 2 completes the second arm of our interferometer. Unlike Mirror 1, which 
is relatively fixed, Mirror 2 will serve as your scanning element - you'll move 
it precisely to change the optical path length in one arm relative to the other.

[Geometric requirement]
The key alignment goal is this: both beams should emerge from the beamsplitter 
parallel to each other after reflection. They need to travel together toward 
the observation screen at exactly the same height and in exactly the same direction.

[Positioning instructions]
Start by positioning Mirror 2 near [-4, -2] on the table. This places it about 
two units from the beamsplitter, creating a roughly equal arm length to Mirror 1. 
Then adjust its rotation so the reflected beam heads back toward the beamsplitter.

[Rotation considerations]
Mirror 2 is mounted perpendicular to the incoming beam, but unlike Mirror 1's 
z-axis orientation, this mirror needs a 90-degree rotation around the z-axis 
because it's in the x-direction arm rather than the y-direction arm.

[Why parallelism matters]
For interference to occur when the beams recombine, they must be traveling in 
the same direction. If the beams emerge at different angles, they won't overlap 
properly at the screen, and you won't see clear interference fringes.

[Degrees of freedom]
You have considerable freedom with Mirror 2 - you can adjust its position along 
X, Y, and Z axes, as well as rotate it around all three axes. This flexibility 
lets you achieve the precise alignment needed for good interference.

[Preparation for next steps]
Once both mirrors are aligned and both beams emerge parallel, we'll add a 
diverging lens to spread the beam, then bring both spots to overlap at the 
screen where interference will become visible.
