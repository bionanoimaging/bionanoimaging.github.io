LVL1_TASK8_DIVERGENT_CALIBRATION - Zero-OPD Calibration with Divergent Light Narration Script
==============================================================================================

[The grand finale technique]
Now we demonstrate the most powerful alignment trick in interferometry: using 
divergent light to find the absolute zero optical path difference position. This 
is how professional optical technicians calibrate their instruments.

[Recap of the problem]
With parallel light, you see interference fringes at ANY mirror separation. The 
fringe pattern might get coarser or finer as you move a mirror, but it never 
disappears completely. This makes it impossible to identify which fringe corresponds 
to exactly equal path lengths.

[How divergent light solves this]
When you use divergent light from our lens, different rays hit the mirrors at 
different angles. Each ray therefore has a slightly different total path length 
through the system. At most mirror positions, these varying path differences mean 
that constructive interference for some rays coincides with destructive interference 
for others, washing out the overall pattern.

But at the exact zero-OPD position - where both arms have identical lengths - 
ALL rays interfere constructively simultaneously, regardless of their angle. This 
creates a burst of strong, visible fringes that clearly marks the reference point.

[The experimental procedure]
In this task, Mirror 1 has been deliberately offset by 15 centimeters. Your job is 
to scan Mirror 2 along the Z-axis while watching the screen. Move slowly and 
carefully. At most positions, you'll see nothing or very faint patterns. But when 
Mirror 2 reaches the position where its arm length matches Mirror 1's arm length, 
suddenly bright colored fringes will appear.

[What to look for]
Start with Mirror 2 around Z = -3.0 units, then slowly increase toward Z = -2.6. 
Watch for the moment when fringes suddenly become visible. That transition point 
marks the zero optical path difference condition with extreme precision.

[Color effects]
If you're using white light instead of monochromatic laser light, you'll see 
colored fringes - rainbow patterns radiating from the center. Each wavelength 
has its own slightly different zero-OPD position, creating beautiful chromatic effects. 
This is why it's sometimes called "white-light interferometry."

[Real-world applications]
This calibration technique is used everywhere precise distance measurement matters:
• In semiconductor manufacturing for measuring film thicknesses
• In astronomy for stellar interferometry and adaptive optics  
• In medical imaging with optical coherence tomography (OCT)
• In gravitational wave detectors like LIGO for maintaining arm balance

[Why this matters for metrology]
Once you've found the zero-OPD position using divergent light, you can switch 
back to parallel light for measurements while knowing your absolute reference 
point. This combination gives you both coarse positioning capability and fine 
measurement precision.

[Completing the lesson]
By completing this task, you've learned not just how to align an interferometer, 
but how to calibrate it to an absolute reference. You now understand a technique 
used by scientists and engineers working on cutting-edge optical systems worldwide.

[Culmination]
This demonstrates the power of combining geometric optics (the lens) with wave 
optics (interference). Understanding both perspectives is essential for mastering 
modern optical instrumentation.
