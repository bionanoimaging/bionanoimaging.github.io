LVL1_TASK6_OVERLAP - Beam Overlap at Screen Narration Script
=============================================================

[Bringing it all together]
Now both beams from the interferometer arms reach the screen, but you'll notice 
they appear as two separate spots. Our next challenge is to make them overlap 
perfectly so they can interfere with each other.

[Why overlap is necessary]
For light waves to create interference patterns, they must occupy exactly the 
same space at exactly the same time. Think of it like throwing two stones into 
a pond: the ripple patterns only interact where the waves physically overlap. 
Similarly, light beams must superimpose to create bright and dark fringes.

[Current state]
Right now, the two beams emerge parallel from the beamsplitter but are slightly 
separated laterally. They're heading in the right direction, but they need to 
be coaxial - traveling along the same axis.

[Alignment technique]
Fine-tune the tip and tilt of Mirror 2 - that means small adjustments to its 
rotations around the x and y axes. These tiny angular changes steer the beam 
slightly at the screen without disrupting the overall alignment of the system.

[What to look for]
Watch the screen carefully as you make adjustments. The two red laser spots 
should gradually move closer together until they merge into a single spot. 
When perfectly overlapped, the combined spot will be brightest in the center.

[Premium mode note]
In the full version with interference enabled, once the beams are truly 
overlapping you should begin to see faint interference fringes - alternating 
bright and dark bands or concentric rings, depending on the exact mirror angles.

[Physical analogy]
Imagine holding two flashlights aimed at a wall. If they're pointed at slightly 
different spots, you see two circles of light. Only when you aim them at exactly 
the same spot do the beams combine. That's what we're achieving here, but with 
much greater precision because we're dealing with coherent laser light.

[Next step]
Once the beams overlap, we can enter the realm of precise wave optics and start 
observing and analyzing interference patterns.
