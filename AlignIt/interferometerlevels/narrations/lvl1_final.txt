Congratulations! You have successfully built, aligned, and calibrated a complete

[Summary of what you accomplished]
Let's review the key techniques you mastered:

First, you learned step-by-step construction - starting with precise laser alignment, 
then installing the beamsplitter at exactly 45 degrees, then setting up both mirrors 
for optimal retroreflection.

Second, you discovered the importance of beam parallelism - making sure both arms' 
beams emerge from the beamsplitter traveling together toward the observation plane.

Third, you practiced fine angular control to achieve circular Haidinger fringes, 
which tell you the mirrors are perfectly perpendicular.

Most importantly, you learned the divergent-light calibration technique - using 
a simple lens to find the absolute zero optical path difference position. This 
is the secret weapon of professional interferometrists.

[Historical significance]
Albert Michelson developed this instrument in the 1880s, refining it over decades. 
His most famous experiment, done with Edward Morley in 1887, used a Michelson 
interferometer to search for the luminiferous ether - the hypothetical medium 
through which light was thought to propagate. Their null result - finding no 
ether whatsoever - helped pave the way for Einstein's theory of special relativity.

Michelson received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1907, the first American to win 
a science Nobel. His interferometer became the basis for defining the meter in 
terms of the wavelength of light, connecting our units of length to fundamental 
constants of nature.

[Modern applications]
The Michelson interferometer is more relevant today than ever:

• LIGO (Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory) uses kilometer-scale 
  Michelson interferometers to detect ripples in spacetime caused by colliding 
  black holes billions of light-years away. The sensitivity is extraordinary - 
  they measure changes smaller than one ten-thousandth the diameter of a proton.

• Optical coherence tomography (OCT) uses white-light interferometry to create 
  three-dimensional images of biological tissues, especially the retina. Millions 
  of OCT scans are performed yearly in ophthalmology clinics worldwide.

• Semiconductor manufacturing relies on interferometry for measuring film thicknesses 
  with nanometer precision, ensuring chip quality.

• Atomic force microscopy and surface profiling use interferometric techniques to 
  map surface topography at the atomic scale.
